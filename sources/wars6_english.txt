1	Mary, of the house of Jesse, a village called Bethlehem, signifies the house of David, renowned for both lineage and wealth. Fleeing with the remainder of the crowd to Jerusalem, she was besieged. The tyrants seized her other possessions, which she had gathered from the Perahia, and brought them into the city. However, the remaining assets of the infant, and anything that might have been daily sustenance, the soldiers would seize whenever they raided. Yet, the noble woman's indignation was fierce, and she often cursed and reproached the thieves for their actions against her.
2	The man neither accelerates nor shows pity and seizes her. To find something for sustenance for others, there was already scarcity, and even to find it became difficult. The famine, however, rages through hearts and veins, and the famine more than the hearts is gnawing at the souls. Taking counsel from her anger, she is driven to nature, and speaking, she said, "Wretched one, in war, famine, and turmoil, who will keep you safe?" To the Romans is slavery, even if we live among them, but even slavery the famine imposes, the rebels are harsher for both.
3	Bring me sustenance and strife for the rebels, and let a life-saving story be the only thing lacking in the misfortunes of the Jews. And at the same time speaking, she was killing her son, afterward cooking half of him, and hiding the rest. Immediately, the rebels arrived, seizing the unprotected portion, threatening that if she did not produce the cooked part, they would kill her immediately.
4	She concealed the remaining part of the child from them, maintaining that a beautiful custom should be preserved. Immediately, fear and awe overcame them, and they vanished from sight.
5	The child is mine, she said, this is a holy child and my work. Eat, for I have nourished him. Do not become more tender than a woman or more compassionate than a mother. If you are righteous and turn away from my sacrifice, I have nourished him, and the rest let it remain with me. After this, those who trembled departed, fearful and reluctant to part with this food from the mother, yet immediately the entire city was filled with dread, and each one receiving the affliction as if it had dared to happen to him. However, a great blessing was upon those who arrived before hearing and seeing such terrible things.
6	Quickly, too, the event was reported to the Romans. Some disbelieved, some wept, many more were induced to hate the nation even more.
7	Emperor Caesar was offering peace and autonomy, and amnesty for all who had rebelled, instead of unity, he proposed a war, and before harmony and peace, famine; and those who were starting to burn our sacred temple, he deemed worthy of such food. However, he would cover the abomination of infanticide with the corpse of his own country and leave no city visible on the globe where mothers nourish their children in this way. Before mothers, it should be handed over to fathers. Those who had endured such things continue to remain in their weapons. At the same time, he also considered the judgment of the men; for it was not likely that those who had suffered on these matters would change their minds if they did not suffer.
8	The walls of Lysas and the towers of Beth-horon were to be attacked by the armies at dusk on the eighth month. For six days previously, the hardest of all stones, Jericho, had beaten on the wall without any response, but the size and harmony of the stones were superior. The gate of the north was hardened and many, weary from pushing the stones, managed to move them. However, under the pressure from within and standing firm, the gate endured, until the siege works reached the organs and the gates of the walls. The Jews were unable to stop them, but when they approached, they fought and those who ascended were thrown back, while those who stood in defense were repelled. Many of the ascenders were killed by those behind them with their spears, while those who approached the gates were cut down with swords. Some of the attackers, having filled their shields with stones, turned them upside-down and dropped them from above.
9	There was not a small number of their murders. Those who brought forth the signs about them were warring, considering it a terrible thing and a disgrace for these matters. In the end, the Jews kept the signs, and those ascending they were destroying. The others, sorrowing for the suffering of the slain, were leaving. However, no Roman was an active participant in their deaths, but those who had previously fought the battles, including Eleazar, brother of the tyrant Simon.
10	Titus, upon seeing the destruction taking place on foreign altars and the impending slaughter, commanded the soldiers to seize the gates.
11	In that situation, Ananus the uncircumcised, Simon's spear-bearer, the most purple, and Archaelous son of Magaddatus, hoping for forgiveness since, with the Jews in control, they were leaving.
12	Titus also was brought before the council for this entire affair, and being bound by necessity, he was compelled to say to them that he had not voluntarily been present, and that they were not worthy of salvation who were already burning the country through their actions.
13	Faith, nevertheless, controlled his emotions, and forgave the men, yet did not appoint them on equal footing with others.
14	The soldiers approached the gates with the fire, and the silver was quickly thrown into the wooden pyre to feed the flames. The Jews, observing the fire surrounding the bodies, were struck with astonishment, and none could act to defend or extinguish. However, those standing by looked on without intervening. Although sorrowful because of the expenditure, they nevertheless maintained their composure for the most part. But when the temple was set ablaze, they directed their anger towards the Romans.
15	The fire continued on that day and throughout the night. Not everywhere, however, were they able to pull down the walls.
16	The following command was given by Titus regarding the removal of the stones and the making of a passage to an easier ascent for the ranks: He himself summoned the commanders. And when six of the foremost had assembled, Tiberius Alexander, the commander-in-chief of all the armies, and Sextus Cerealis, the leader of the fifth cohort, and Larkius Lepidus, the leader of the tenth, and Titus Frigius, the leader of the fifteenth, and Phronto, a military commander of the two cohorts from Alexandria, and Marcus Julianus, the overseer of Judea, and with them the presidents and thousand commanders gathered, it was decided about the temple. To the former it seemed fit to follow the law of war: For the Jews would never cease to fortify the temple, which was the source of their gathering. However, some considered if the Jews would leave the temple unguarded and no one would arm against it, it would be saved; but if they fought and entered, it would be burned. For it was no longer a temple, but a fortress, and the rest was to be the work of those forcing the impiety, not theirs.
17	Titus, however, the Jews, not entering upon him to fight, said they would defend the unprotected and would never attempt such a task; for the damage would be to the Romans, just as their dominion remained; but they were already confident and had proposed to Phronto, Alexander, and Kerialis. Then, therefore, the council was dissolved and all the forces commanded to cease, instructing the commanders to use the chosen ones from the sowers to pave the way through the waste places and to extinguish the fire.
18	The forces of the Jews, on that day, were struck with paralysis and surprise, gathering strength and breaking out through the eastern gate towards the guards of the outer sanctuary around the second hour. However, they firmly met their attack, forming a wall-like line with their shields, and it was clear they were not yielding easily to the multitude of those rushing at them nor to their spirits being overcome. But when Caesar reached the engagement, for he had observed from Antonia, he encouraged with select horsemen.
19	Jews could not hold their ground, but those falling first turned to retreat and face the Romans. However, changing their formation, they escaped again until, by the fifth hour of the day, the Romans had forced them into lockdown within the temple.
20	The fire from within was kindled by God, and on the tenth day of the lunar month of Loos, which had previously been stirred up by the Babylonian king, the flames took their origin and cause.
21	The insurrectionists, having momentarily withdrawn at the arrival of Titus, are again set against the Romans. The guards of the temple become a sign to those quenching the inner sanctuary's fire, who, having turned the Jews, followed them up to the temple.
22	The guards, without waiting for orders nor fearing such danger, seized with some supernatural impulse the burning flame, having been extinguished by a fellow soldier with a golden door, through which entry to the house near the temple from the northern climate was possible. When the fire was raised among the Jews, a cry of affliction worthy arose, and they rallied to the defense, neither fearing to live anymore nor preserving their strength, for which reason the watchmen were previously weakened.
23	A man ran and reported to Titus. He, finding himself in the tent resting after the battle, sprang up and approached the temple, extinguishing the fire. However, the commanders followed behind, and the ranks followed that which was terrified. There was a cry and uproar, due to the overwhelming power that was chaotically stirred.
24	The emperor himself commanded those fighting to extinguish the fire, neither did they heed his voice with greater outcry, nor did they obey the commands of his hand. Some fought while others were enraged. Among the troops, neither exhortation nor threat restrained their impulse, but the spirit of all was their commander. Many fought among themselves, and some, still heated and stirred by the falling stones, suffered defeat. Near the temple, those of the emperor's command were ordered not to enter, but those before them were urged to set the fire. The insurgents' plan was already to aid, but slaughter and chaos prevailed everywhere. From the people, a weak and unarmed one who was caught was slaughtered, and around the altar, a multitude of corpses was guarded. However, the bodies of those slain in the temple fell beneath the foundations.
25	Emperor himself, being unable to quell the fervor of the troops and the fire prevailing, entered inside with the commanders to view the holy temple and what was in it, much more renowned was the report among the foreigners than the reputation among his own. However, the fire was not yet put out from any place, the houses surrounding the temple, thinking that they could still save the work, rushed in, and the emperor himself tried to urge the troops to extinguish the fire and commanded Libealian, centurion of his bodyguards armed with spears, to punish the disobedient. However, the spirits and the fear of the one who was restraining, the hatred towards the Jews, and a fierce battle fury prevailed, and many were driven by the hope of plunder, thinking that all inside was full of riches, and seeing the golden decorations outside. Then someone from those who had come inside the temple, leaping out during a pause in the fighting, threw the fire into the windings of the temple gate in darkness. Then, suddenly, the fire appeared from within, and the commanders with the emperor retreated, and no one was allowed to approach from outside.
26	The temple was thus violently overthrown by Caesar.
27	The overthrow of the temple, as previously mentioned, was accomplished by Caesar. It would be difficult for anyone, examining and hearing, to find anything more marvelous in terms of construction, size, cost, and the sanctity associated with it. Even in this period, the precision is evident: a month and a day were preserved, in which, as was said, the temple was rebuilt by the Babylonians. From its first construction, which Solomon the king established, up to its recent destruction, which occurred in the second year of the reign of Jehoiachin, a total of 1,130 years can be calculated, with seven and nineteen months remaining. However, from the time it was rebuilt, which occurred in the second year of the reign of Cyrus, up to its capture by Nebuchadnezzar, there are 364 years and forty-five days.
28	The burning temple attracted plunder, and no mercy or pity was shown, neither for age nor for the sanctity, but both children and the elderly, the poor and the priests were slaughtered alike, and every kind of person was swept away by the war, equally those who pleaded and those who resisted. The flame ascended to its greatest height, and due to the height of the hill and the size of the burning structure, anyone seemed to be setting the city on fire. Yet nothing could be imagined to be greater or more terrifying than the sound of that cry. There was confusion among the Roman units, and the cries of the rebels, surrounded by fire and iron, echoed. The crowd above the hill groaned for the city. However, the sudden change of the crowd towards the enemies and towards the suffering echoed a mournful sound.
29	Already many, famished and covering themselves as they saw the fire of the temple, again groaned and lamented. The nearby hills and the city of Peria intensified the sound.
30	The fear of the tumult was greater. From the very roots of the sacred hill, one could scarcely escape the advancing fire, but the blood of the fire was more numerous than those being slain. No trace of the slain was visible, but the soldiers, diving into the storehouses, flung out the bodies of those fleeing. The rebellious crowd, having attacked the Romans, scarcely went out from the temple and into the city, while the remnant of the citizens fled to the outer portico. Some of the priests, first casting their weapons and seats made of marble into the Romans, then, when neither a way nor the fire opened for them, threw themselves into the fire and set the temple ablaze. Myrhos, son of Belgas, and Joseph, of Dalaios, were among them. The Romans, deeming the fire of the temple to be unimportant, indiscriminately destroyed everything, consuming the remaining goods in the stores and the gates except for two, the eastern and the western. They later destroyed these as well.
31	The treasure chests, containing an innumerable amount of money, innumerable clothing and various valuables, were plundered, as every Jew who could, took from there the houses of the well-off.
32	And they came also to the outer storeroom of the temple. From the crowd, women and children and a mixed multitude fled into tens of thousands. Before Caesar made a decision regarding them or commanded the leaders, the soldiers, driven by their emotions, took hold of the storeroom. Some, throwing themselves into the fire, were consumed by it, while others remained in it. However, no one was saved from such a multitude. The cause of their destruction was a false prophet who announced that day to those in the city that God was calling upon the temple and accepting the signs of salvation. There were many prophets then who were close to the people, waiting for the help from God, proclaiming that even the heavy burdens and guards would be relieved, and hope would be fulfilled. But a man is quickly persuaded in distress, and when even the terrifying things holding him are removed by deception, then the one suffering becomes completely without hope.
33	The wretched populace were persistently deceived and lied to by those falsely claiming to be gods, while the clear and obvious impending destruction was neither heeded nor believed by them. Yet, as if mute and without senses, they gave no heed to the proclamations of God. First, when over the city a star resembling an aster appeared and stood for a year, a comet. Second, before the secession and the onset of war, as the people gathered for the feast of unleavened bread, it was the seventh month, the month of Xanthicus. Nightly, at the ninth hour, such bright light enveloped the altar and the temple that it seemed like a bright day, and this continued for half an hour.
34	The object that seemed good to some was immediately captured, to the priests however, for those departed, it was closed immediately. And according to the same festival, a cow was led to the altar and gave birth to a lamb in the middle of the temple. The eastern gate of the inner sanctuary, made of bronze and extremely heavy, was barely opened by men, and with iron chains it was secured with deep holes dug into the unyielding stone. It opened automatically at the fifth hour of the night. The temple guards reported this to the commander, and he barely managed to close it. This seemed best to the common people, for they believed it would open the God to them. The wise, however, feared the security of the temple being breached, and they saw the sign of desolation within themselves. After the festival, not many days later, on the first and fifth day of Artemisius month, a great demonic appearance was seen. It would seem to be a terror if not also reported by those who saw it and the events that followed the signs. Before sunset, chariots and troops were seen moving through the sky and surrounding the cities. During the festival, which is called Pentecost, the priests, according to their custom for the rituals, entered the inner sanctuary and first reported shaking and a sound. After that, we hear a multitude of voices from here. The more terrifying part, however, is that a certain Jesus, a son of Ananias, a layman from the countryside, before the four-year war when the city was most peaceful and thriving, came to the festival where everything was customarily offered to God in the temple. Suddenly, a voice came from the east, from the west, from the four winds, from Jerusalem and the temple, from brides and bridesmaids, from the whole people. This was heard day and night by all the narrow-minded. However, some of the prominent citizens, angered by this blasphemous event, seized the man and subjected him to numerous beatings.
35	The man, neither speaking on his own behalf nor to the fleeing ones, continued the cries which he had previously made. However, the leaders, believing it to be more demonic, led him before the procurator of the Romans.
36	The stone, hard as it was, neither cried out nor expressed any response, but as if echoing strangely, it answered each blow given to Jerusalem. When Albino asked him who he was and from where, and why he was speaking these things, he remained silent about these things, but regarding the weeping over the city, he did not cease. Albino, recognizing madness in him, released him.
37	The stone, hard as it was, neither appeared nor spoke to any of the citizens, but it seemed to lament strangely each day for Jerusalem. It did not also express any response to those who struck it, nor did it bless those who gave it food, but there was always a mournful echo. Especially in the festivals it cried out. This continued for seven years and five months without a break, neither softening its voice nor stopping, until it saw the siege works of the city. Going around from the walls, again and again it lamented over the city, the people, and the temple. But as its final act, it released those same clanging sounds and its soul, struck by a stone thrown at it, immediately died.
38	The one who conceives this will find the God of men watchful and always revealing the salvific things to his own generation, but the others, under the influence of ignorance and evil, perishing, where the Jews and the temple, after the profanation by Antonia, made a four-cornered object, as it is written in the scriptures to capture the city and the sanctuary when the temple becomes a four-cornered object. The oracle for war, found ambiguous in the sacred writings, was especially applied to them. However, the oracle was a matter of debate among the wise, and the prophecy of the Overseer (Vespasian) was proven true when he became autocrat of Judea. But it is not possible for humans to escape or avoid what is destined. The leaders and the ones who judged the signs, some for pleasure and others with contempt, were exposed to their own ignorance when the capture of their homeland and their own destruction was demonstrated.
